Where the depth
actually comes from
Before the MBA, before Dawell, before any of this — I spent six years trying to become an IES officer. I cleared the Prelims. I appeared in the Mains. The final result didn't go my way.

The UPSC Engineering Services exam is one of India's most demanding competitive processes. It requires mastering Mechanical Engineering at depth, alongside economics, policy, law, and general studies — simultaneously, for years. I studied 10+ hours a day, structured my own curriculum, tracked my own progress, and kept going without external validation or guaranteed outcomes.

When it didn't work out, I applied to IIM Nagpur. I got in. And I carried everything those six years had built — into every case study, every sprint, every client conversation I've had since.

The analytical depth I bring to requirements gathering, the composure I have in high-pressure project deliveries, the ability to hold complexity without getting overwhelmed — none of that came from an MBA classroom. It was already there.
